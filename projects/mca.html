Multicriteria Analysis for Urban Tree Planting

A weighted overlay workflow graphic identifying priority sites for tree planting in Hong Kong to reduce urban heat exposure.

MCA workflow: criteria, data sources, GIS analysis steps, and decision map output. Grey nodes indicate hard exclusions.

What is multicriteria analysis?

Multicriteria analysis (MCA) combines several spatial criteria into a single map so that a final decision can account for many factors at once rather than just one. Most urban planning or environmental questions cannot be answered using a single dataset, so MCA offers a solution. A flood map alone, for example, cannot show where buildings are or where pollution is highest. MCA standardizes each criterion onto a common scale, assigns it a relative weight, and merges them into a single output surface.

GIS makes this combination possible at a spatial scale. Distance, elevation, air pollution, and population density can each exist as their own layer, letting GIS software reclassify the values of each and combine them using the raster calculator into one output layer. MCA is typically used wherever decisions involve trade-offs between competing values, such as identifying sites for urban development, prioritizing land for conservation, or locating new public transportation or health infrastructure (Malczewski & Jankowski, 2020).

The problem

The workflow identifies which neighborhoods in Hong Kong should be prioritized for new trees or greenery to reduce urban heat exposure, focused on areas that need it most rather than areas that are simply easiest to plant. Residents in dense, low vegetation, lower income neighborhoods often experience the worst heat exposure while having the least private outdoor space to fix it themselves. Urban planners need sites that are physically capable of supporting trees, not filled with concrete or underground infrastructure, and likely to produce a real cooling benefit once planted. MCA fits this problem because the best site for a tree is not simply the warmest spot, but the hottest spot that is also feasible to plant and serves the people who need it most.

The criteria

Seven criteria drive the analysis. Land surface temperature, from thermal satellite bands, is treated as the core issue. Existing vegetation prioritizes areas with little to no greenery, largely overlapping with the urban core. Population density prioritizes higher density areas, since cooling benefits need to reach people. Age and income prioritize areas with more elderly or lower income residents, who are more vulnerable to heat illness and less likely to have air conditioning or the option to leave during a heat wave. Available space and land ownership both act as hard cutoffs, excluding sites with no physical room or no valid access agreement regardless of how well they score elsewhere. Distance from existing green space prioritizes areas farther from a park or street tree, since new green space does more good where nothing is currently nearby.

The data

This workflow draws on Landsat or Sentinel-2 thermal imagery for surface temperature, the same Sentinel-2 NDVI data used in the vegetation project, population density by district from the Hong Kong Census and Statistics Department, age and income data per district, land use and underground utility records, land ownership records, and a parks and tree inventory from the Leisure and Cultural Services Department. Some of this data may be privatized and harder to access, and age and income data would ideally come at a finer resolution than district level, since district boundaries in a city as dense as Hong Kong can lump wealthier and poorer blocks together.

The GIS workflow

Data preparation starts with reprojecting everything into a shared coordinate system and clipping to the study area, following a similar process to the NDVI project at a larger scale, with thermal and NDVI imagery matched to the same season. Each layer is then reclassified onto the same 1-5 scale so temperature, vegetation, population, vulnerability, and distance from green space can be compared directly, with temperature ideally handled by percentile rather than a fixed degree cutoff given how much climate varies across Hong Kong.

Weights are then assigned to each layer, with temperature and vulnerability weighted most heavily, followed by population and distance to green space. Testing different weight combinations helps confirm reliability, since a neighborhood that keeps appearing near the top regardless of weighting is a stronger result than one that flips entirely under small adjustments. Areas where planting is not an option are excluded first, then the remaining classes are combined by multiplying each layer by its weight and summing them into a final map. The output is sorted into priority tiers, symbolized, and sanity-checked against a regular map to confirm no top site lands somewhere unusable, and compared back against the original NDVI map, since a top priority spot that turns out to be one of the greenest areas in the dataset would suggest an error earlier in the process.
