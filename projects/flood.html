Digital Elevation Modeling and Flood Risk

Where is the region of interest and why?

New Orleans, Louisiana was chosen as the region of interest for these maps. The map itself is centered on the bend in the Mississippi River, with the city and Lake Pontchartrain visible to the north. New Orleans sits largely below sea level in a natural bowl shape between the river and the lake, making it one of the most flood vulnerable places in the world, which made it a compelling case for testing flood simulation techniques.

Method and what was surprising

This project follows the 3D urban flood approach, and has New Orleans being highlighted because of its well documented history of catastrophic flooding. Rather than a full 3D animated display, the result is instead shown in 2D with hillshade added for perspective and depth. What was most surprising was how narrow the elevation range is between dry land and complete submersion. Comparing the 0m, 2m, and 4m scenarios showed a massive change between 0 and 2 meters, but barely any change between 2 and 4 meters, meaning the city does not flood gradually but instead reaches a near total flood state very quickly. For viewing, however, I chose to show the difference between 0 and 4, since the 2 meter scenario is visually very similar to the 4 meter scenario and does not add much to the story.

New skills used

This assignment involved merging multiple raster elevation tiles into a single digital terrain model, since the elevation data was distributed across separate tiles that needed to be combined to analyze the full area of interest. Hillshade rendering and layer blending were also new, adding visual texture and a sense of depth that a flat color stretch could not provide, which mattered here because New Orleans has such a minimal elevation range that a standard stretch does not clearly show features like the levees or the riverbed.

The raster calculator was used to perform the flood simulation itself, generating new raster layers showing which areas would flood at 0, 2, and 4 meters of water. These layers were styled with a unique values symbology to show the flood overlay while keeping the terrain texture visible, allowing for a comparative visualization across the different scenarios rather than a single static elevation map.
